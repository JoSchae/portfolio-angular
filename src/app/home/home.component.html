Webportal for DB-Regio trains

I worked for Germany's biggest train company to provide an offline-capable WebPortal on every regional train, with cloud-based distribution (OpenShift) and with very little real resources on each train. Very challenging, but also very satisfying after we got it to work.

At the start of this project I had already worked at Deutsche Bahn for about a year. The DevOps-Team I was part of got asked to create a solution for a web-portal for all regional trains in germany.

This may sound easy and straightforward, but it's everything but. First of, the hardware on trains is very different, depending on which manufacturer build the train. Second, the hardware is also pretty weak. We had about 1GB of memory and 1Ghz of CPU-Power. The rest was allocated to every other services running on trains. Thirdly is the problem of connection. Not only are the trains often driving through tunnels, but there are also a lot off blind spots (for example in forests), where you just don't have a connection.

With all the problems identified we got to the other requirements our customer wanted. It needed to be orchastrated by a Content-Management-System (at first dotCMS, later on Contentful), and should display not only the newest news from all of germanys news-companies, but also videos. All of that while still loading in under three seconds and (and now this is the really hard part) be always available, regardless if the train has an internet-connection or not.

The solved these problems with three strategies:

  • For the overall installation on the train hardware we used LXC (Linux Containers). That enabled us to have standardized Software and Services on all trains. We also could roll out updates whenever we wanted over the air, instead for having to wait for a maintenance date in a train-garage.
  • Second, we also installed a cache one the train, utlilising all available harddrive space. Those caches were automatically updated every hour, so users wouldn't notice any outdated information/news on the platform. This also solved the "offline"-capability.
  • Last, but not least, we had a big backend cloud-service, mirroring the data on each train, that continuosly pulled the newest data from each news-partner. It was essentially our distributing-node, holding and getting all the data, then distributing it to every train, whenevery they where able to ask for the newest information.

In the end it was one of the most complex projects I was ever part of. We had to mirror train hardware for our development and testing, we had to overview the first test-trains, and we had to completly rethink normal distribution-options, because we had to keep train-hardware & -architecture in mind. A very fun but also challenging project. Also my first experience with leading a team, specifically for this project, in the frontend-department.

CHECK24 Credit Comparsion

Every developers dream - starting a fresh project with the newest frameworks and technology. If only we didn't had to work with every bank and their own APIs. A challenge, that even led to the team being hired to do the same in Austria; after we finished, of course ;)

Germanies biggest comparsion-website, CHECK24, wanted to create a new comparison for credit. They already had a lot of the usual busniess-logic thought out (from the previous website), but now wanted to include new monitorings, algorithems and user-retention-strategies. We were able to start complety from scratch, with only a crude design that relied heavly on the already established company-design. But everything else was a complete new build.

A highly challenging project. We had to work with about every bank-API available in germany, building a beautiful website in line with the rest of CHECK24 style, utilising UX/UI to the best of our abilities for user-retention and still have it working lightning fast (even with the API-calls to get the best credit deals for about 10 different usages). The goal was to, for whatever the user wanted (a credit for a new car, a house, an investment, or even just for pleasure spending), have a website, that guides them to the best possible option available in under 10 minutes.

There was no doubt, that we had to use the newest frameworks. All off the restrictions and busniess-demands practically forced us to utilize the most robust frontend-framework (Angular v14 at that point in time), togehther with a Nest.js middleware(already established in CHECK24) and a custome-build SpringBoot backend (with a database, of course. MongoDB to be precise)

We spend months on just designing the different components and services that would interact with each other. The look of the components was the easy part. The interaction to provide the user with a smooth and speedy experience was the hard one. Almost every screen had three or more states, depending on what the user chose or put in, everything had to be evaluvated, and every banking-API had its own quirks.

In the end it worked out beautifully. A completly new website ind the style of other CHECK24 websites, components that can be used for years to come (because of there generic nature and customizability) and a backend that can withstand thousands of customers simultaniously. It was in fact so good, the team was asked to move to austria. The austria CHECK24-branch also wanted something similar. Sadly that's where I couldn continue, because of the conditions of my contract. But I will always remember those 1.5 years fondly. A good time, a lot of fun with my collegues, and working with the newest frameworks was just awesome. And there also was the "Doner-Kebap-Thursday" :D

AMAG.ch

Creating a wonderful User-Experience while being constrained by a CMS is challenging. Leading a team, consulting for the customer, and working with legacy code even more so. Let me tell you about the biggest automotive company in Switzerland.

If I would disregard my work for the DB Regio Webportal, this is the most restrictive project I ever worked on. AMAG is the main automotive distributer in switzerland. Around 2019 they bought in AEM (Adobe Experience Manager) as a CMS for their websites. Every since than, Trueinnovation has build, supported and consulted on any websites and projects AMAG had.

The team I'm leading for this project consists of 2 Frontend-Developer, a Tester, a Backend-Developer/Software-Architect (funnily enough, he's my supervisor, but let me lead the team and reclused him from almost every software-development, because he had so many other projects to also supervise) and me. We constanly work on either new features for the main business of AMAG (automobile services in their on-site garages all over switzerland) or new side-ideas. At the time of writing that's a completly new website to aquire cars through a subscription-model and as of now a new way to report any damages to your car and shedule repairs.

The car-subscription project was a doozy. We had to work with a lot of other departments, integrated a completly new microservice, and gurantee data-consistency with every other already implemented database/service (like the SAP system in the background). All the while with working in the rather constricting environment of a CMS. But in the end, we were able to do, what we set out to. As the senior lead, I wanted this "practically" new website to not have the same flaws of the rest of the organically grown monolith I was first introduced to when I joined the company. Clean code, service and component encapsulation, easy to understand and refactore logic and simple ways to test & document everything.

The damage-report project was even better. We not only were able to just start with a fresh code-base, but also had an opportunity to establish a completly new pattern of generating future components. The design was almost 1:1 the same as the biggest already existing service amag.ch offers. Because of that, we set out to do this design, but this time as generics. Now, you can just use the templates whenever you want and have them look, feel and behave exactly the same. Now, we can implement specific content into that design whenever we want and write specific business-logic for whatever the customer wants to archive. I would say it is the (for a CMS) cleanest code I ever produced with a team, and everyone of us grew with the project. It was also a lot of fun to mentor my two junior frontend-developers and also talk architectur with my supervisor. While he had the last say, he mostly just agreed with mee, and it was a pleasure to also flex my architectur-certificate for backend for the time I'm employed at trueinnovation.
